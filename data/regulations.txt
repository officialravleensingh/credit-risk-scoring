SECTION 1: CREDIT RISK ASSESSMENT STANDARDS
The Basel III framework requires financial institutions to maintain a minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital ratio of 4.5% of risk-weighted assets. Credit risk models must be validated regularly and must demonstrate discriminatory power through metrics such as the Gini coefficient and ROC-AUC score. Internal Rating-Based (IRB) approaches must estimate Probability of Default (PD), Loss Given Default (LGD), and Exposure at Default (EAD) for each borrower segment.

SECTION 2: DEBT-TO-INCOME RATIO GUIDELINES
The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) recommends that lenders evaluate the borrower's debt-to-income (DTI) ratio as a key affordability indicator. A DTI ratio below 36% is generally considered acceptable for most loan products. Borrowers with a DTI ratio between 36% and 43% may still qualify but require additional scrutiny. A DTI ratio above 43% is a significant risk indicator and typically disqualifies borrowers from qualified mortgage products under the Ability-to-Repay rule. Lenders must document their DTI assessment methodology.

SECTION 3: CREDIT SCORE THRESHOLDS AND RISK CLASSIFICATION
Credit scores (FICO scale 300-850) are used to classify borrower risk. Scores of 800 and above are classified as Exceptional and qualify for the best interest rates. Scores between 740 and 799 are Very Good and qualify for competitive rates. Scores between 670 and 739 are Good and represent the average borrower. Scores between 580 and 669 are Fair and indicate elevated risk. Scores below 580 are Poor and represent high default risk. Lenders are required under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) to provide adverse action notices when denying credit based on credit score.

SECTION 4: FAIR LENDING AND ANTI-DISCRIMINATION REGULATIONS
The Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) prohibits discrimination in credit decisions based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, age, or receipt of public assistance. The Fair Housing Act (FHA) extends these protections to mortgage lending. Lenders must ensure that credit scoring models do not have disparate impact on protected classes. Regular fair lending audits are required. The Community Reinvestment Act (CRA) requires banks to meet the credit needs of all segments of their communities, including low- and moderate-income neighborhoods.

SECTION 5: DELINQUENCY AND DEFAULT RISK STANDARDS
A loan is considered delinquent when a payment is 30 or more days past due. Loans 90 or more days past due are classified as non-performing. Lenders must maintain loan loss reserves based on expected credit losses under the Current Expected Credit Loss (CECL) standard. Borrowers with two or more delinquencies in the past 24 months represent elevated risk. Public records such as bankruptcies, tax liens, and civil judgments are strong negative indicators and must be considered in credit decisions. Chapter 7 bankruptcy remains on a credit report for 10 years; Chapter 13 for 7 years.

SECTION 6: EMPLOYMENT AND INCOME VERIFICATION REQUIREMENTS
Lenders must verify income and employment status as part of the Ability-to-Repay (ATR) rule under the Dodd-Frank Act. Acceptable income documentation includes W-2 forms, tax returns, pay stubs, and bank statements. Self-employed borrowers must provide two years of tax returns. Unemployed borrowers must demonstrate alternative income sources such as investments, retirement income, or alimony. Employment stability of at least two years in the same field is considered a positive indicator. Retired borrowers may use pension income, Social Security, and investment distributions as qualifying income.

SECTION 7: LOAN-TO-VALUE AND LOAN AMOUNT GUIDELINES
The loan-to-value (LTV) ratio measures the loan amount relative to the asset value. For personal loans, lenders assess the loan amount relative to annual income. A loan amount exceeding 3x annual income is considered high risk. For mortgage loans, an LTV above 80% typically requires private mortgage insurance (PMI). Jumbo loans exceeding conforming loan limits require stricter underwriting standards. Loan purpose affects risk assessment: debt consolidation loans carry moderate risk, while business loans carry higher risk due to income variability.

SECTION 8: INTEREST RATE AND LOAN TERM RISK FACTORS
Higher interest rates on a loan may indicate that the borrower was assessed as higher risk at origination. Loan grades (A through F) reflect the lender's internal risk assessment, with Grade A representing the lowest risk and Grade F the highest. Longer loan terms (60 months vs 36 months) increase total interest paid and extend the lender's exposure period. The installment-to-income ratio should not exceed 15% of monthly gross income for unsecured personal loans. Variable rate loans carry additional risk due to payment uncertainty.

SECTION 9: RESPONSIBLE LENDING AND CONSUMER PROTECTION
The Truth in Lending Act (TILA) requires lenders to disclose the Annual Percentage Rate (APR), total finance charges, and total payment amount before loan consummation. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) governs how lenders may collect on delinquent accounts. Predatory lending practices, including excessive fees, loan flipping, and equity stripping, are prohibited under various state and federal laws. Lenders must provide borrowers with a copy of their credit report if adverse action is taken based on credit information. The Right to Financial Privacy Act protects borrower financial records from unauthorized disclosure.

SECTION 10: OPEN ACCOUNTS AND CREDIT UTILIZATION
Credit utilization, defined as the ratio of current balance to total credit limit, is a significant factor in credit risk assessment. A utilization rate below 30% is considered healthy. Utilization above 50% indicates potential financial stress. The number of open accounts reflects credit breadth; having between 3 and 10 open accounts in good standing is generally positive. Too many recently opened accounts may indicate financial distress or credit-seeking behavior. Closed accounts in good standing continue to positively influence credit history for up to 10 years.

SECTION 11: REGULATORY CAPITAL AND PROVISIONING
Under IFRS 9 and CECL standards, financial institutions must recognize expected credit losses at loan origination rather than waiting for actual default. Stage 1 assets (performing loans) require 12-month expected credit loss provisioning. Stage 2 assets (significant increase in credit risk) require lifetime expected credit loss provisioning. Stage 3 assets (credit-impaired) require lifetime ECL with interest calculated on the net carrying amount. Stress testing under DFAST and CCAR frameworks requires institutions to model credit losses under adverse and severely adverse economic scenarios.

SECTION 12: LENDING DECISION FRAMEWORK
A comprehensive lending decision must consider five key factors: Character (credit history and repayment behavior), Capacity (income and DTI ratio), Capital (assets and net worth), Collateral (security for the loan), and Conditions (loan purpose and economic environment). This framework, known as the Five Cs of Credit, provides a structured approach to credit underwriting. Automated credit decisions must be explainable and auditable. Human review is required for borderline cases and for any decision that may have disparate impact on protected classes.
